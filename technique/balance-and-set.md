# Balance and Set

"Set" is the rowing term for the lateral balance of the boat — keeping it level side to side. In sculling, particularly the single (1x), maintaining set is a constant active task. Unlike a sweep boat where crew weight helps stabilize, a single scull is entirely the sculler's responsibility.

> **Analogy:** Balancing a single scull is like riding a bicycle — it feels impossible at first, then becomes automatic with practice. The key in both cases is to stay relaxed and make small corrections rather than overcorrecting.

---

## What Controls Balance

Balance in a sculling boat comes from two sources:

1. **Your body position** — sitting centered on the seat, even weight on both feet, core engaged
2. **Your handle heights** — the oar handles act as a lever system. Even a small difference in handle height tips the boat significantly

Both must be correct simultaneously. Handle heights are the more common culprit in beginners.

---

## Body Position for Balance

**Sit centered:**
- Equal weight on both sit bones — not leaning or tilting to one side
- Both feet flat and even in the foot stretchers
- Core gently engaged — not rigid, but not collapsed
- Shoulders level and relaxed — not hunched or raised

**Head position:**
- Keep your head up and eyes forward
- Looking down or turning your head sharply can tip the boat — especially important to remember when steering

**Avoid:**
- Tilting your pelvis or leaning to check your blade
- Tensing up when the boat tips — this usually makes it worse. Relax and make small corrections

---

## Handle Heights and the Lever System

Each oar extends roughly 2.5× further outside the oarlock than it does inside (the outboard is much longer than the inboard). This means:

- **1 cm change in handle height = approximately 2.5 cm change in blade tip height**

Small handle adjustments have large effects on blade height and therefore on boat balance. This is why consistency of handle heights through the full stroke cycle is so important.

**At the finish (blades squared and buried):**
- Handles should be roughly a thumb's width from the lower ribs
- The boat should be level — check this by looking at the waterline on both sides

**During the recovery (blades feathered, in the air):**
- Maintain consistent downward pressure on both handles
- The blades should travel at a uniform height off the water — not wobbling up and down
- Left hand slightly above right (see [Bladework](bladework.md) for hand overlap)

**During the drive:**
- Keep the handle path as horizontal as possible
- Avoid arcing the handles upward or downward through the stroke

## Stability vs. Balance

These are related but different concepts:

| | Stability | Balance |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | The boat's resistance to tipping | The boat actually being level |
| **Affected by** | Hull width, water conditions, boat weight | Handle heights, body position, blade control |
| **In a stable boat** | Easier to correct errors | Errors still need correcting |
| **In an unstable boat** | Errors punished immediately | Requires more precision |

Wide-hulled training singles are more stable (forgiving of handle height errors) than narrow racing shells. As a beginner, starting in a stable hull is strongly recommended.

## Practical Drills for Balance

### 1. Blades-on-water recovery
Row normally but on the recovery, keep the feathered blades resting lightly on the water surface. Use them as stabilizers. This removes the pressure of balancing in the air and lets you focus on the stroke sequence.

Progress: reduce how much you rely on the water contact until the blades skim above the surface.

### 2. Arms-only rowing
Sit with legs extended and row using only the arms. No leg drive, no body swing. This isolates upper body control and hand heights. Excellent for beginners learning to feel the boat.

### 3. One-oar rowing circles
Row with one oar only, making circles on the water. This teaches you how individual handle height affects the boat's lean, and builds confidence with one side at a time.

### 4. Pause drills
Pause at key positions in the stroke (hands away, arms-and-body, half slide) and check: is the boat level? Are your handles even? Are your shoulders relaxed? Use the pause to self-correct before continuing.

### 5. The "dead safe" position
When stationary and uncertain, hold both handles in one hand with your arm extended, oars perpendicular to the hull and blades feathered on the water. This is a stable resting position that gives you time to compose yourself.

## Tips for Beginners

- **Relax.** Gripping the handles tightly when nervous actually makes balance worse. Tension transfers through the oars into the boat. Breathe and soften your grip
- **Make small adjustments.** When the boat tips, a tiny nudge on one handle corrects it. Large overcorrections create oscillation
- **Don't look down at the water.** Trust the feel of your handles and focus your eyes forward
- **Slow down.** Lower stroke rates give you more time to think and correct. Balance improves with time, not speed
- **Use training wheels.** Many clubs have pontoon attachments that clip onto the riggers of a single. These are excellent for first outings
- **Film yourself.** A short video of yourself rowing makes handle height inconsistencies immediately visible

---

## How Balance Develops Over Time

Balance is a proprioceptive skill — your nervous system learns to make micro-corrections automatically, without conscious thought. This takes repetition on the water, not just reading about it.

A rough progression:
1. **Early sessions:** Focus is almost entirely on not capsizing. Normal
2. **After ~5–10 sessions:** Boat feels less chaotic; you notice what causes tips
3. **After a season:** Balance becomes background awareness, freeing attention for technique
4. **Long-term:** You row balanced without thinking about it, even in choppy water

> The single scull is the ultimate balance teacher — it gives immediate, honest feedback on every mistake. Embrace it.
