# Bladework

Bladework is the art of controlling the oar blade through the air and water — feathering, squaring, catching, and extracting cleanly. It is one of the most sculling-specific skills and has no equivalent on the erg.

> **Analogy:** Think of bladework like gear-shifting in a car. Done smoothly, it's invisible and efficient. Done poorly, it creates friction, disrupts rhythm, and costs speed.

---

## The Two Blade Positions

**Squared (vertical):** The blade face is perpendicular to the water surface — ready to push against the water and propel the boat. The blade should be squared just before the catch.

**Feathered (flat/horizontal):** The blade is rotated 90 degrees so it lies parallel to the water surface. Used during the recovery to reduce wind resistance and allow the blade to pass low over the water without catching.

---

## Grip: The Foundation of Bladework

Good bladework starts with how you hold the handles. Poor grip makes feathering and squaring extremely difficult.

**How to hold the handle:**
- Hold the handle in your **fingers**, not your palm. The fingers should "hook" over the handle
- Thumb rests across the end of the handle (not underneath it)
- Wrists stay **flat** throughout the stroke — a bent or dropped wrist is the most common grip error
- Keep the grip **loose** — a tight grip causes forearm fatigue and prevents smooth blade rotation

**Hand overlap in sculling:**
- Unlike sweep rowing, sculling handles cross in the middle of the stroke
- The **left (port) hand** rows slightly *above* the right hand throughout the stroke
- To avoid collisions, the left wrist brushes over the back of the right wrist as the hands cross — this also helps keep hand heights even and the boat balanced

> **Tip:** Hold the handle the way you'd hold a bird — firm enough that it can't escape, loose enough that you don't crush it.

---

## Feathering

Feathering is the rotation of the blade from vertical (squared) to horizontal (flat) as it exits the water at the finish.

**How to feather:**
1. As the blade lifts out of the water (tap down), the **inside wrist drops** — this rotates the handle and flattens the blade
2. You will feel a small "click" as the oar settles into the feathered position in the oarlock
3. The **outside wrist stays flat** — it does not participate in the feathering motion
4. The feather happens *as* the blade exits the water, not after

**Common feathering errors:**
- Dropping the outside wrist — causes the blade to wash out before it's fully extracted
- Feathering too late — the blade is still in the water when you try to rotate, which is inefficient and hard on the wrists
- Feathering too early — the blade gets pulled sideways while still connected to the water
- Breaking the wrist upward instead of keeping it flat

> **Drill:** Practice feathering stationary at the dock. Hold the oar in the water at the finish position and practice the tap-down + wrist rotation until it feels natural, before trying it while moving.

---

## Squaring

Squaring is the rotation of the blade from flat (feathered) back to vertical (squared) in preparation for the catch.

**When to square:**
- Begin squaring as the legs near full compression on the recovery — before you reach the catch position
- The blade should be fully squared and ready to enter the water by the time you arrive at the catch

**How to square:**
- The inside wrist rises, rotating the handle — the reverse of the feathering motion
- The blade rotates from flat back to perpendicular
- Keep the grip relaxed — let the oar roll from your fingers into your palm as you square

**Common squaring errors:**
- Squaring too late — arriving at the catch with the blade still feathered means a slow, messy entry
- Squaring too early — a squared blade in high wind creates resistance on the recovery
- Gripping too tight — prevents the smooth rotation needed to square efficiently

---

## The Catch Entry

A clean catch is where bladework meets the drive phase. The blade must enter the water fully and find pressure *before* the leg drive begins.

**The motion:**
- A slight upward pressure on the handles drops the blades cleanly into the water
- The blade should be **fully covered** (the top edge just at or below the surface)
- The entry should be quick and decisive — around 0.1–0.12 seconds for a good sculling catch

**Blade depth:**
- Too shallow: the blade washes out during the drive, losing power
- Too deep: the blade dives, making extraction difficult and putting strain on the lower back
- Correct depth: fully covered, with the shaft horizontal to the water

---

## The Extraction (Tap Down)

The extraction is how the blade leaves the water cleanly at the finish.

**The motion:**
- Push the handles **down** slightly — this levers the blade *up* and out of the water
- Feather simultaneously as the blade exits (see above)
- A clean extraction makes no sound — no splash, no dragging

**Common extraction errors:**
- Rowing the blade out horizontally instead of tapping it up
- Feathering while the blade is still in the water (creates drag and loses boat run)
- A splashy or turbulent extraction — each splash is energy bled from the boat

> **Listen to your blades.** A quiet finish means a clean extraction. Splashing or dragging sounds are immediate feedback that something is wrong.

---

## Square Blade Rowing (Drill)

Rowing with the blades kept permanently squared (never feathered) during the recovery is one of the most powerful drills in sculling. It forces:
- Correct hand height (the blade will hit the water if handles are too low)
- Clean extraction (you can't hide a messy finish)
- Better balance (requires consistent handle heights)

Start by doing just a few strokes square, then return to feathering. Build up to longer square blade sequences as confidence grows.

**Progression:**
1. 3 strokes square, 1 stroke feathered
2. 10 strokes square, 10 feathered
3. Full lengths square

> **Resource:** [Faster Masters – Square Blades Challenge](https://fastermastersrowing.com/square-blades-episode-3) — a three-episode series with progressions and drills.

---

## Key Drills Summary

| Drill | What It Trains |
|---|---|
| Stationary feathering at dock | Basic wrist rotation without the complication of movement |
| Square blade rowing | Clean entry/exit, handle height, balance |
| Arms-only rowing | Blade depth and hand path |
| Pause at hands-away | Recovery sequencing and bladework timing |
| One-oar circles | Feathering and squaring in isolation on one side |
