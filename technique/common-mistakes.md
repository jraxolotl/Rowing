# Common Mistakes

This page catalogues the most frequent technical errors made by beginner scullers, explains why they happen, and describes how to fix them. Most beginners make all of these at once — that's normal. Work on one at a time.

---

## 1. Pulling with the Arms Before the Legs Drive

**What it looks like:** The arms bend and start pulling the handle toward the body immediately at the catch, before the legs have pushed.

**Why it happens:** Instinct. Pulling feels like the natural way to move a boat. The legs-first sequence is counterintuitive until you've drilled it repeatedly.

**Why it's a problem:** The arms are small muscles; the legs are enormous. Pulling with the arms first wastes your biggest power source and overloads the back and shoulders. It also shortens the effective stroke length.

**The fix:**
- Drill arms-only rowing (legs flat, no body swing) to feel the difference between arm pull and leg drive
- Cue: "Push the footboard away" rather than "pull the handle"
- Pause at the catch and consciously initiate with the legs before allowing the arms to bend

---

## 2. Rushing the Slide (Recovery)

**What it looks like:** The seat rockets forward toward the catch immediately after the finish, with no controlled deceleration.

**Why it happens:** Beginners feel the urgency to "get back" for the next stroke. Also, without strong knee flexor control, the slide tends to roll fast.

**Why it's a problem:** Rushing forward shifts body weight toward the bow, which pushes the boat's stern down and disrupts the boat's run. It also means you arrive at the catch off-balance and unprepared. At higher stroke rates, it makes it nearly impossible to take a clean catch.

**The fix:**
- Row at very low stroke rates (14–18 SPM) and exaggerate the pause at arms-away and body-over
- Count: "1 (drive), 2-3-4 (recovery)" to feel the correct ratio
- Cue: "Let the boat come to you" — at the catch, the boat is moving toward you; you don't need to rush toward it

---

## 3. Incorrect Drive Sequencing (Body Before Legs)

**What it looks like:** The back swings open (leans back) immediately at the catch, before the legs have driven — sometimes called "shooting the slide" or "bum shoving."

**Why it happens:** The back muscles fire first because they feel powerful, or the rower is unconsciously trying to create length by leaning back early.

**Why it's a problem:** The back opens too soon and loses its mechanical advantage before the legs have done their work. The stroke becomes short and back-heavy, greatly increasing injury risk.

**The fix:**
- Hold the body angle fixed at the catch and drive the legs, letting the body remain in the forward lean position until the legs have pushed about halfway
- Cue: "Hold the body, push the feet" at the catch
- Film yourself from the side — the back-before-legs fault is immediately visible on video

---

## 4. Gripping the Handles Too Tightly

**What it looks like:** White knuckles. Rigid wrists. Difficulty with feathering. Forearm fatigue early in the row.

**Why it happens:** Nervousness, or a natural protective instinct to hold on firmly when the boat feels unstable.

**Why it's a problem:** A tight grip transmits tension into the oars, disrupts bladework, and leads to forearm/wrist pain. It also prevents the smooth rotation needed to feather and square the blade cleanly.

**The fix:**
- Consciously soften the grip on every recovery
- Hold the handles in the fingers, not the palm (see [Bladework](bladework.md))
- Cue: "Hold a tube of toothpaste — firm enough not to drop it, not so hard that toothpaste comes out"
- On the recovery, let the handle rest in your fingers with minimal pressure

---

## 5. Over-Compressing at the Catch

**What it looks like:** Shins are pushed past vertical at the catch — the rower is too compressed, with the chest nearly to the knees.

**Why it happens:** A mistaken belief that more compression equals more length and therefore more power.

**Why it's a problem:** Shins past vertical put the legs in a mechanically weak position for the initial drive. It also tips the body weight forward, making the catch position unstable and harder to hold.

**The fix:**
- Stop the slide when the shins reach vertical
- If flexibility allows a slightly more compressed position without rocking forward on the seat, that's fine — but shins past vertical is the line to watch
- Cue: "Shins vertical at the catch"

---

## 6. Collapsing the Chest / Hunching the Back

**What it looks like:** Rounded shoulders and a collapsed lower back, especially at the catch and through the drive.

**Why it happens:** Core fatigue, or a habit of sitting hunched in everyday life that transfers to the boat.

**Why it's a problem:** A rounded back loses its mechanical strength and puts the spine in a vulnerable position during the drive. It also reduces reach at the catch and limits power transfer from legs through the trunk.

**The fix:**
- Focus on sitting tall — "chest up, shoulders down and wide"
- Engage the core gently throughout the stroke — not rigidly, but not collapsed
- In sculling, keeping the chest up at the catch (rather than collapsing forward) actually allows more effective leg drive and better blade control
- Cue: "Proud chest at the catch"

---

## 7. Messy Extraction / Splashy Finish

**What it looks like:** The blades exit the water with a splash, throwing water up or to the sides.

**Why it happens:** Feathering too late, insufficient tap-down, or extracting the blade by pulling it horizontally rather than lifting it out.

**Why it's a problem:** Each splash is wasted energy. A messy finish disrupts the boat's run and tells the water you're done pushing — prematurely.

**The fix:**
- Practice the tap-down motion: push the handle down to lever the blade up
- Feather as the blade exits — the rotation should happen simultaneously with extraction, not after
- Cue: "Quiet finish" — listen for silence; splashing is your feedback that something went wrong
- Drill: square-blade rowing forces cleaner extraction by removing the feather from the equation

---

## 8. Unequal Handle Heights

**What it looks like:** One blade dragging on the water during recovery; the boat consistently leaning to one side.

**Why it happens:** Subtle asymmetry in grip or arm position, or simple inattention to hand heights.

**Why it's a problem:** Unequal handles cause the boat to tip. A blade dragging on the water creates significant drag — hundreds of times more resistance than the blade in the air.

**The fix:**
- Consciously check handle heights on every recovery, especially during the hand-crossing overlap
- Remember: left hand slightly above right throughout the stroke
- Drill: square-blade rowing makes handle height errors immediately apparent (the lower blade will touch the water)
- Film yourself from behind — height asymmetry is easy to spot from this angle

---

## 9. Steering Problems (Veering Off Course)

**What it looks like:** The boat curves consistently to one side, or the rower doesn't notice they've drifted until they're nearly at the bank.

**Why it happens:** Uneven pressure between the two oars, and/or not checking the course frequently enough.

**Why it's a problem:** You can't stay in your lane, and in traffic you could collide with other boats.

**The fix:**
- Pick a landmark far ahead (a dock, a tree, a buoy) and row toward it; check it every few strokes
- To steer: apply slightly more pressure on the oar on the side you want to turn away from
- When checking the course, look over one shoulder without moving your body — this takes practice to do without tipping the boat
- Cue: Start at arms-and-body-away on the recovery before looking — you'll have the most stability at this moment

---

## 10. Holding the Breath

**What it looks like:** The rower turns red, feels dizzy, and tires far faster than expected.

**Why it happens:** Concentration and tension in a new skill environment suppresses breathing automatically.

**Why it's a problem:** Rowing is aerobic exercise — you need continuous oxygen supply. Breath-holding also increases core tension, which paradoxically makes balance harder.

**The fix:**
- Exhale on the drive (when effort is highest); inhale on the recovery
- Some rowers prefer two breaths per stroke at lower ratings — find a pattern and stick to it
- Cue: "Breathe out as you push" — make the exhale audible until the habit forms

---

## A Note on Mistakes

Every experienced sculler has made all of these errors — many still make some of them under pressure. The difference between a beginner and an experienced sculler isn't the absence of faults; it's the ability to notice and correct them quickly.

Use each outing to focus on one error at a time. Trying to fix everything at once fixes nothing.
