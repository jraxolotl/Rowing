# Boat Types

Sculling boats — called **shells** — come in several configurations depending on the number of rowers and the boat's purpose. Understanding the different types helps you choose the right boat for your stage of learning and your goals.

---

## Overview of Sculling Boat Classes

| Class | Abbreviation | Rowers | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single scull | 1x | 1 | The most common training and racing boat for individual scullers |
| Double scull | 2x | 2 | Two rowers, each with two oars |
| Quadruple scull | 4x | 4 | Four rowers, each with two oars; usually coxless |
| Coxed quad | 4x+ | 4 + cox | A cox steers and calls; less common in club rowing |

The **x** in the abbreviation always indicates sculling (each rower has two oars). A **+** means the boat is coxed.

---

## The Single Scull (1x)

The single is the defining sculling boat — one person, two oars, entirely self-reliant.

**Why beginners eventually train in a single:**
- Forces you to develop balance, bladework, and boat control independently
- Immediate and honest feedback — every error is felt directly
- Flexible training — go out whenever you want, at your own pace
- The "teacher" of the sport: many coaches say you learn more in a month of singles than a year in a crew boat

**Why beginners don't start in a racing single:**
- Racing shells are extremely narrow and unstable — they will tip you immediately
- The margin for error is very small until balance is developed

### Training Singles vs. Racing Singles

| | Training Single | Racing Single |
|---|---|---|
| **Hull width** | Wider (more stable) | Very narrow |
| **Stability** | Forgiving — good for learning | Unforgiving |
| **Speed** | Slower | Faster |
| **Weight** | Heavier | Lighter (carbon fiber) |
| **Pontoons** | Can be fitted | Not applicable |
| **When to use** | First season(s) | Once balance is solid |

Many clubs also fit training singles with **pontoons** (float attachments on the riggers) for absolute beginners — these act as training wheels and prevent capsizing during the very first sessions.

## The Double Scull (2x)

Two rowers, each with two sculling oars.

**Characteristics:**
- More stable than a single (the combined weight lowers the center of gravity)
- Synchronization between the two rowers is essential — timing mismatches are immediately felt
- A great intermediate step between a stable training single and a competitive single
- Useful for training with a more experienced partner who can provide on-water coaching

**Seating:** In a double, one rower sits closer to the bow and one to the stern. The stern rower typically steers (by applying uneven pressure) and sets the rhythm.

---

## The Quadruple Scull (4x)

Four rowers, each with two sculling oars.

**Characteristics:**
- Very stable due to combined crew weight and wider hull
- Fastest of the sculling classes at elite level (faster than eights in some conditions)
- Excellent for club training — stable platform for developing technique in a team environment
- The "stroke seat" (closest to the stern) sets the rhythm; all others follow

**4x vs. 4x+:** Most club and competitive quads are coxless (4x). A coxed quad (4x+) is rarer and usually seen in junior or masters programs where navigation safety is prioritized.

## Boat Construction Materials

| Material | Common Use | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Fiberglass** | Club/training boats | Durable, heavier, lower cost |
| **Carbon fiber** | Racing/performance boats | Very light, stiff, expensive, less forgiving of bumps |
| **Wood (cedar/mahogany)** | Traditional/recreational | Beautiful, heavier, requires maintenance |
| **Kevlar/composite** | Mid-range | Good durability/weight balance |

As a beginner, you will almost certainly row a club-owned fiberglass or composite training single. Owning a personal boat is a longer-term consideration.

## Key Boat Anatomy Terms

Understanding the parts of the boat helps you communicate with coaches and club members.

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Hull** | The main body of the boat |
| **Bow** | The front of the boat (the direction of travel) |
| **Stern** | The back of the boat (where the rower faces) |
| **Port** | Left side (when seated facing stern) |
| **Starboard** | Right side (when seated facing stern) |
| **Gunwale** | The upper edge of the side of the hull |
| **Keel** | The centerline running along the bottom of the hull |
| **Rigger** | Metal or carbon frame extending from the hull, holds the oarlock |
| **Oarlock / Gate** | The swivel fitting that holds the oar and acts as the pivot point |
| **Pin** | The vertical post at the end of the rigger around which the oarlock rotates |
| **Slide / Track** | The rails that the seat rolls along |
| **Seat** | The sliding seat that moves on the tracks |
| **Foot stretcher** | Adjustable footboard where the feet are secured |
| **Fin / Skeg** | A small keel attachment underneath the hull that aids straight tracking |
| **Saxboard** | The narrow deck area along the side of the hull near the gunwale |
| **Deck** | The covered area at bow and stern |

## Choosing a Boat as a Beginner

You will not need to choose a boat for a while — most clubs provide boats for learn-to-scull programs. When the time comes to think about your own boat, consider:

1. **Stability first** — a wider, more stable hull is far more appropriate than a fast racing shell until your balance and bladework are solid
2. **Fit matters** — boat sizing depends on your weight. A hull that is too large for you will sit too high in the water; too small and it will sit too low
3. **Used boats are fine** — good used fiberglass training singles are widely available and much cheaper than new carbon racing shells
4. **Ask your club** — most clubs have strong opinions on which boats suit which rowers, and can help you make the right choice
