# Rigging

Rigging is the process of adjusting a boat's hardware — rigger height, oarlock pitch, spread, foot stretcher position, and oar settings — so that it fits the rower and maximizes efficiency and comfort.

> **Analogy:** Rigging is like fitting a bicycle. The mechanics are the same for everyone, but saddle height, handlebar position, and gear ratios need to be tuned to the individual rider. A poorly fitted bike is uncomfortable and inefficient; a well-fitted one feels like an extension of your body.

**As a beginner:** You don't need to rig your own boat right away. Most clubs set up training boats to a standard configuration. However, understanding rigging helps you communicate problems to your coach and make sense of what you're feeling in the boat.

---

## Key Rigging Measurements

### 1. Spread (Pin-to-Pin Distance)

**What it is:** The distance between the two oarlock pin centers, measured across the beam of the boat.

**Why it matters:** Spread determines the arc of the oar through the water and affects how heavy or light the oar "load" feels. A wider spread lightens the load; a narrower spread increases leverage but feels heavier.

**Typical range for sculling:** 157–161 cm  
**Common starting point:** 160 cm

Taller scullers with wider shoulders may prefer a slightly wider spread; smaller scullers may prefer a narrower setting.

---

### 2. Gate Height (Oarlock Height)

**What it is:** The vertical distance from the lowest point of the seat to the lowest inside point of the oarlock.

**Why it matters:** Height determines where your hands sit during the drive. Too low and you can't extract the blade cleanly; too high and the oar washes out during the drive.

**Typical range:** 160–175 mm above the seat  
**Left gate slightly higher than right** (to accommodate the hand overlap in sculling — typically 6–12 mm differential)

**Beginner tip:** Start with 12 mm or more differential and reduce it as technique improves.

**Quick height check on the water:**
1. Sit at the finish position with blades squared and buried
2. Let the oar find its natural depth
3. The handle should be roughly a thumb's width from your lower ribs
4. If handles feel too high or low, the gate height needs adjusting

---

### 3. Pitch

**What it is:** The angle of the oarlock face away from vertical. Pitch causes the blade to "climb" slightly during the drive, preventing it from diving too deep.

**Why it matters:** Too little pitch and the blade dives deep and is hard to extract; too much pitch and the blade rides too shallow and washes out.

**Standard setting:** 4 degrees (built into most modern oarlocks)  
Most beginners will not need to adjust pitch — it is factory-set on new equipment.

---

### 4. Foot Stretcher Position

**What it is:** The horizontal and vertical position of the footboard where your feet are secured.

**Why it matters:**
- **Horizontal (fore-aft):** Determines how far your seat travels relative to the oarlock pin. If positioned too far forward or back, you won't achieve the correct compression at the catch
- **Vertical (heel height):** Affects the angle of your ankle at the catch. A heel height of 16–18 cm from the seat is a common starting point

**Quick check:** At full compression (catch position), your shins should be approximately vertical. If you can't reach this without your heels lifting excessively, the stretcher may need moving.

---

### 5. Oar Settings (Inboard and Overlap)

**Inboard:** The length of the oar from the handle end to the collar (the plastic stop that rests against the oarlock). Inboard determines the leverage ratio.

**Common sculling inboard:** ~87 cm  
Increasing the inboard makes the oar feel lighter; decreasing it makes it feel heavier.

**Overlap:** The amount by which the handles cross each other at the midpoint of the stroke. This is a function of spread and inboard.

**Recommended overlap:** 12–20 cm  
Taller scullers with longer arms typically use more overlap; shorter scullers less. This is a personal preference once the basics are established.

## Rigging for Beginners: Starting Points

If you are setting up a boat for the first time, these are reasonable starting values for an average-sized adult:

| Setting | Starting Value |
|---|---|
| Spread (pin to pin) | 160 cm |
| Gate height (seat to oarlock) | 160–165 mm |
| Height differential (L over R) | 12 mm |
| Pitch | 4° (factory default — don't adjust) |
| Inboard | 87 cm |
| Overlap | 14–16 cm |
| Heel height | 16–18 cm |
| Oar length | 286 cm (standard adult sculling oar) |

Ask your club coach to verify these settings before your first outing — they can also adjust for your specific body proportions.

## Signs Your Rigging May Need Adjusting

| Symptom | Likely Cause |
|---|---|
| Blade dives too deep during drive | Too little pitch; or pulling upward on the handles |
| Blade washes out (exits early) | Too much pitch; or gate height too high |
| Handles too high at finish (can't reach lower ribs) | Gate height too high |
| Handles too low at finish (digging into lap) | Gate height too low |
| Can't reach good compression at catch | Foot stretcher too far stern-ward |
| Seat hits stern end of tracks at catch | Stretcher too far bow-ward; or over-compressing |
| Hands collide in the middle | Overlap too large; reduce inboard or widen spread |
| Lower back pain | Often gate height or stretcher position; consult a coach |
| Forearm pain | Often grip-related, but can be affected by inboard |

---

## What to Adjust First (Beginner Priority Order)

1. **Gate height** — has the most immediate impact on comfort and blade control
2. **Foot stretcher position** — affects catch compression and back comfort
3. **Spread** — adjust only after height and stretcher feel right
4. **Inboard/overlap** — fine-tune once the major settings feel good
5. **Pitch** — leave at factory default unless a coach recommends otherwise

---

## Tools You'll Need

- **Tape measure** — for spread, inboard, and foot stretcher measurements
- **Long straight edge or level** — for measuring gate height from seat
- **Pitch meter** — optional; used to check oarlock pitch precisely
- **Small adjustable spanner** — for tightening rigger bolts
- **Spare washers** — most gate height adjustments use plastic spacer washers above and below the oarlock
