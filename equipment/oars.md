# Oars

In sculling, each rower uses **two oars** — one in each hand. These are sometimes called **sculls** to distinguish them from the single sweep oars used in sweep rowing. Understanding your oars helps you rig them correctly, care for them, and choose the right equipment as you progress.

---

## Anatomy of a Sculling Oar

```
Handle ← Inboard → Collar/Button ← Outboard → Sleeve → Blade
```

| Part | Description |
|---|---|
| **Handle** | Gripped by the rower. Usually rubber or synthetic grip. Comes in different diameters (small/medium/large) |
| **Shaft** | The long body of the oar. Modern oars are carbon fiber or fiberglass |
| **Collar / Button** | The plastic stop that rests against the oarlock, defining the inboard measurement |
| **Sleeve** | The plastic section near the collar that sits in the oarlock gate |
| **Blade / Spoon** | The wide flat surface that pushes against the water |

---

## Sculling Oar vs. Sweep Oar

| | Sculling Oar | Sweep Oar |
|---|---|---|
| **Per rower** | 2 (one each hand) | 1 (both hands) |
| **Typical length** | ~284–288 cm | ~370–374 cm |
| **Blade size** | Smaller | Larger |
| **Grip** | One at each end of blade | Both hands on same handle |

---

## Oar Dimensions

### Length

Standard sculling oar length for adults: **284–288 cm**  
Common default: **286 cm**

- Longer oars provide more leverage but require more strength to maintain stroke rate
- Shorter oars are easier to handle and allow higher rates but provide less leverage per stroke
- Taller, stronger rowers may use longer oars; smaller or lighter rowers may prefer shorter

### Inboard

The inboard is the distance from the handle end to the collar (the part that rests against the oarlock).

Standard sculling inboard: **~87 cm**

- Increasing inboard makes the oar feel lighter (more mechanical advantage to the blade side)
- Decreasing inboard makes the oar feel heavier
- Inboard and spread must be set together to achieve the desired overlap

### Blade Types

Modern sculling blades are shaped for efficiency in the water. The two most common types:

| Blade Type | Shape | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Macon / Tulip** | Symmetrical, teardrop | Older style; still used on some club oars |
| **Hatchet / Big Blade** | Asymmetrical, wider | Standard on most modern oars; more surface area, better catch |
| **Smoothie** | Narrower hatchet | Used by some elite scullers for cleaner entry |

Most beginners will use whatever blade type the club provides. The choice between blade types becomes relevant as technique develops.

## Oar Materials

| Material | Use | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Carbon fiber** | Performance/racing | Very light, stiff, responsive; preferred at competitive level |
| **Fiberglass** | Club/training | Heavier than carbon but durable and less expensive |
| **Wood** | Traditional | Rare; still used by some recreational and coastal rowers |

Club boats are most commonly equipped with fiberglass or entry-level carbon oars. Personal carbon oars are an investment made as technique and commitment to the sport develop.

## Overlap

Overlap is the amount by which the two oar handles cross each other at the midpoint of the stroke (when both handles are in front of you during the drive).

**Formula:**  
`Overlap = Inboard + Inboard - Spread`  
`Overlap = (2 × Inboard) - Spread`

**Example:** With an inboard of 87 cm and a spread of 160 cm:  
`Overlap = (2 × 87) - 160 = 174 - 160 = 14 cm`

**Recommended overlap:** 12–20 cm

- Too little overlap: the handles never fully cross, limiting your arc
- Too much overlap: the handles collide uncomfortably at the crossover point

**Hand order at the crossover:**  
Left (port) hand crosses **above** right (starboard) hand. This is the convention throughout the rowing world. The left oarlock is set slightly higher than the right to accommodate this.

## Oar Care and Maintenance

Oars are more durable than they look, but some basic care extends their life:

- **Rinse after salt water** — freshwater rinse removes corrosive salts from the sleeve and collar
- **Store horizontally** — don't lean oars against a wall by the blade; this stresses the shaft
- **Check the collar regularly** — the collar can crack or move; make sure it's secure and at the correct inboard measurement
- **Inspect the blade** — small cracks in fiberglass blades should be repaired before they grow
- **Sleeve wear** — the plastic sleeve that sits in the oarlock wears over time; replace when it becomes grooved or loose
- **Handle grip** — worn handles can be re-wrapped with grip tape or replaced

## Buying Your Own Oars

Most beginners row with club-provided oars. When the time comes to consider personal oars:

1. **Get rigged first** — know your ideal inboard, blade type preference, and oar length before buying
2. **Ask your coach** — they can advise on what suits your style and physique
3. **Major manufacturers:** Concept2, Croker, Dreher, Braca, Empacher
4. **Used oars** — good condition used oars are widely available and significantly cheaper than new
5. **Match to your boat** — the oar specifications should be compatible with your boat's rigging

---

## Oar Setup Checklist

Before each outing, quickly verify:

- [ ] Collar is at the correct inboard measurement and firmly in place
- [ ] Sleeve is not worn through or cracked
- [ ] Blade is not cracked or delaminating
- [ ] Handle grip is secure
- [ ] Both oars are the same length and inboard (confirm after any adjustments)
