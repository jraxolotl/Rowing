# Stroke Phases

Every sculling stroke cycles through four phases in a continuous loop. Mastering the sequence — and the transitions between phases — is the foundation of good sculling.

```
CATCH → DRIVE → FINISH → RECOVERY → (repeat)
```

Think of it like a heartbeat: each phase flows into the next, and no single phase works in isolation.

---

## The Catch

The catch is the moment the blades enter the water and the drive begins. It is the most technically demanding micro-moment in the stroke — everything that follows depends on getting this right.

**Body position at the catch:**
- Shins vertical (or as close to vertical as comfortable)
- Arms fully extended toward the stern
- Body leaning slightly forward from the hips, chest up (not collapsed)
- Core engaged, back straight — not hunched
- Shoulders relaxed and wide, not scrunched up
- Head neutral, eyes forward

**What happens:**
- The blades are squared just before the catch (see [Bladework](bladework.md))
- A slight lift of the hands drops the blades cleanly into the water
- The catch should be quick and precise — a good entry takes around 0.1–0.15 seconds
- The goal is to connect the blade face to the water *before* the leg drive starts, not after

**Common catch errors:**
- "Diving in" — starting the leg drive before the blade is connected. You lose power and damage your back
- Over-compressing — shins past vertical puts the legs in a weak position
- Reaching with the shoulders instead of the hip hinge — shortens effective stroke length
- A "jabby" or rushed entry — forces the blade and creates turbulence

> **Coaching cue:** "Catch on the recovery" — prepare the blades early and let them drop in cleanly. Almost no one puts the blades in too *early*; almost everyone puts them in too *late*.

---

## The Drive

The drive is the power phase — the only part of the stroke that actually propels the boat. The sequence of muscle engagement is critical.

**The order: Legs → Body → Arms**

1. **Legs first:** Push through the heels. The legs are the biggest, strongest muscles — they generate around 45% of total stroke power. The back and arms hold their position while the legs fire
2. **Body swing:** Once the legs have driven about halfway, the trunk begins to open (swing back) from the hips
3. **Arm pull:** When the legs are nearly straight and the body has opened, the arms draw the handles toward the lower chest/upper abdomen

**What the boat feels:**
- During the drive, the boat decelerates slightly as the rower moves toward the stern
- The blade should remain at consistent depth throughout — fully covered, not diving deep or washing out
- The handle path should be horizontal — not arcing up or down

**Common drive errors:**
- Pulling with the arms before the legs have fired — "shooting the slide"
- Breaking the back early instead of holding body angle while legs press
- Handles traveling in an arc rather than a horizontal line
- Grip too tight — leads to forearm fatigue and prevents clean rotation at the finish

> **Coaching cue:** "Legs, body, arms" — say it to yourself every stroke until it becomes automatic.

---

## The Finish

The finish is where the drive ends and the blades exit the water. A clean finish sets up the recovery; a messy one costs speed.

**Body position at the finish:**
- Legs fully extended
- Body leaning back slightly past vertical (roughly 20–25 degrees)
- Handles drawn to the lower ribs / upper abdomen
- Elbows bent and drawn past the body
- Wrists flat

**The extraction (tap down):**
- Push the handles **down** slightly to lift the blades out of the water — this is called the "tap down"
- As the blades exit, rotate the handles to feather (flatten) the blades
- The extraction should be clean and quiet — no splash, no dragging

**Common finish errors:**
- Dropping the wrists to feather — causes the blade to "wash out" before it's fully extracted
- Over-drawing (pulling the handles too far into the body or past horizontal)
- "Winging" the elbows out after the extraction — wastes time and disrupts flow
- Rushing to move arms away before the blade has fully left the water

> **Coaching cue:** "Tap and away" — the tap down extracts the blade; then immediately send the arms away toward the stern.

---

## The Recovery

The recovery is the non-propulsive phase — the return to the catch position. Its purpose is to reset for the next stroke while disturbing the boat's momentum as little as possible.

**The order: Arms → Body → Legs (the reverse of the drive)**

1. **Arms away first:** Extend the arms fully toward the stern before the body moves at all. This is the most common sequencing error in beginners — many move body and arms simultaneously
2. **Body rocks over:** Once the arms are extended, hinge forward from the hips — the body swings over toward the catch position
3. **Slide:** Only after the body angle is set do the knees begin to bend and the seat rolls forward toward the catch

**Key recovery principles:**
- The recovery should be *slower* than the drive. At low stroke rates, aim for roughly a 2:1 ratio (twice as long recovering as driving)
- Keep the blades a consistent height off the water — low enough for control, not dragging
- Decelerate the seat as it approaches the catch — don't rush into frontstops
- The recovery is where you breathe and mentally prepare for the next catch

**Common recovery errors:**
- Rushing the slide — the most common beginner error; disrupts rhythm and tires you faster
- Moving body and arms together instead of sequentially
- Letting the knees rise before the arms are extended (the hands get blocked)
- Arriving at the catch unbalanced or with momentum still carrying the seat forward

> **Coaching cue:** "Arms, body, slide" — repeat on every recovery until the sequence is locked in muscle memory.

---

## Putting It Together: Rhythm

A well-timed stroke has a clear two-part rhythm: a **short, powerful drive** and a **longer, controlled recovery**. The boat glides on the recovery — rushing it wastes that glide.

At a relaxed training pace (18–22 strokes per minute), you should feel as though you have plenty of time on the recovery. As rate increases, the recovery shortens — but the sequence never changes.

**The stroke cycle in one sentence:**  
*Catch clean → legs → body → arms → tap down → arms away → body over → slow slide → repeat.*
