## What is Sculling?

Sculling is a form of on-water rowing where each rower uses **two oars** (one per hand), as opposed to sweep rowing where each rower holds one oar with both hands.

### Boat types in sculling

| Abbreviation | Boat | Rowers |
|---|---|---|
| 1x | Single scull | 1 |
| 2x | Double scull | 2 |
| 4x | Quadruple scull | 4 |
| 4x+ | Coxed quad | 4 + cox |

Sculling in a single (1x) is ideal for training on your own schedule. Doubles and quads introduce teamwork and synchronization.

### Sculling vs. Erging

| | Sculling | Erging (Indoor Rowing) |
|---|---|---|
| Setting | On water | Indoor machine |
| Oars | Two (one per hand) | One handle |
| Balance | Active challenge | Not required |
| Bladework | Core skill | Not applicable |
| Steering | Required | Not applicable |
| Best first step | Learn-to-row program | Can self-teach with videos |

## Getting Started

Sculling has a steeper initial learning curve than erging because of boat balance. **In-person instruction is strongly recommended before going out alone.**

### Suggested beginner progression

1. **Stability** — learn to keep the boat set (balanced) before focusing on power
2. **Bladework** — feathering, squaring, clean catches and releases
3. **Power application** — leg drive, body swing, arm pull in the correct sequence
4. **Rhythm and rate** — blending it all into a fluid, efficient stroke
5. **Racing skills** — varied stroke rates, starts, race tactics

> Source: [Faster Masters Rowing – Advice to the New Sculler](https://fastermastersrowing.com/advice-to-the-new-sculler/)

### Finding a learn-to-row program

- **USRowing Club Finder:** https://usrowing.org/clubs — search for clubs offering sculling programs near you
- Most clubs offer a structured learn-to-scull course (typically 4–8 sessions) in stable training singles, sometimes fitted with pontoons
- Expect to cover: basic stroke mechanics, safety, traffic patterns on the water, and how to navigate a single

## Technique

### The four phases of the sculling stroke

```
CATCH → DRIVE → FINISH → RECOVERY
```

| Phase | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Catch** | Blades enter the water; knees bent, shins vertical, arms extended, body prepared |
| **Drive** | Power phase — legs push first, then body opens, then arms pull |
| **Finish** | Blades exit the water cleanly; legs extended, handle drawn to lower ribs |
| **Recovery** | Arms extend, body swings forward, knees bend — set up for next catch |

**Key principle:** The drive order is **legs → body → arms**. The recovery order reverses: **arms → body → legs**. Many beginners rush the recovery; use it to breathe and reset.

### Sculling-specific skills (not on the erg)

- **Feathering:** rotating the oar handle so the blade lies flat on the water during recovery — reduces wind resistance and catches on choppy water
- **Squaring:** rotating the blade back to perpendicular just before the catch
- **Hand overlap:** in sculling (unlike sweep), the oar handles cross in the middle — the left (port) hand leads slightly over the right
- **Balance / set:** keeping the boat level by maintaining equal pressure on both oars through the stroke
- **Steering:** using one oar more than the other to steer; in a single there is no cox — you navigate yourself

### Common beginner mistakes

- Pulling with arms before the legs have driven — robs you of power
- Rushing the slide back to the catch — disrupts rhythm and tires you faster
- Sitting up too tall at the catch — reduces effective blade depth and length
- Gripping the handles too tightly — causes wrist and forearm fatigue
- Over-compressing at the catch — puts knees in a weak position for leg drive

One of the best free sculling video resources available. Topics include:
- Grip and hand position

## Equipment

### Boat types for beginners

- **Training single (stable / wide hull):** ideal for learning — slower but much more forgiving
- **Racing single (narrow hull):** faster but very tippy — not recommended until you have solid balance
- **Double (2x):** a great next step after the single; a more experienced partner can help stabilise the boat

### Key equipment terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Shell** | The boat itself |
| **Oar / Scull** | The blades (you have two in sculling) |
| **Oarlock / Gate** | The swivel fitting that holds the oar |
| **Foot stretcher** | Adjustable footplate in the boat |
| **Slide** | The rails your seat rolls on |
| **Rigger** | Metal frame extending from the hull, holds the oarlock |
| **Feather** | Rotating the blade flat during recovery |
| **Square** | Rotating the blade perpendicular for the catch |

### Rigging basics

Rigging refers to adjusting the hardware (oarlock height, spread, pitch) to suit your body proportions. This significantly affects comfort and efficiency. Ask your coach or club to help set up your rig before your first rows.

## Competitions & Racing

### Race formats

**Regattas (sprint racing)**
- Standard distance: 2,000m
- Side-by-side racing in lanes
- Typical season: spring–summer

**Head races**
- Time trial format over a longer course (typically 3–5km)
- Crews start at intervals and are timed
- Typical season: autumn–winter

### Boat class notation
Sculling boats are marked with an **x** in the abbreviation (e.g. 4x = quad scull). A **+** means coxed, **−** means coxless.

### Useful racing resources
- **Henley Royal Regatta:** https://www.hrr.co.uk — the most prestigious sculling regatta in the world
- **FISA / World Rowing:** https://worldrowing.com — international governing body, rules and results
- **USRowing:** https://usrowing.org — national governing body (US)
- **British Rowing:** https://www.britishrowing.org
